Pokémon Random Battle Predictor
Executive Summary

Group Members: T. Daniels, X. Huang, G. Knapp, M. Mannan, M. Newman

Introduction

Pokémon battles are turn-based games in which two players compete with teams of 6 Pokémon. These matches are played both casually and competitively, and the large number of different Pokémon and unique game rulesets allows for enormous variability in gameplay and strategy.

One popular game format on Pokémon Showdown ("Showdown") is the "random battle", where players compete with teams of six Pokémon randomly generated at the start of each match. Ideally, generated teams should be evenly balanced, so that, going into a battle, both players have nearly equal chances of winning. We consider the distributions of the teams' Pokémon's statistics, types, and relative advantage over one another to see if these features are well balanced across opposing teams. If the construction algorithm is balanced, comparison of these features should not strongly indicate a player's chance of winning.

Data Collection

Showdown logs a large amount of information about each match played on the site, allowing for a full, turn-by-turn reconstruction of each match from its publicly available replay JSON files. Using a web-scraper making API requests, we saved approximately 16k Showdown match replays. We then deployed a custom text parser via a Battle class to compile details such as match length, Pokémon used, and player Elo ratings.

The Seed Extraction Hurdle
In order to learn the complete teams of six in each match—including those not fielded before the match ended—it was necessary to locate the pseudorandom strings used as seeds in generating players' random teams. This string was fed back into Showdown's server source code to re-generate the complete team configurations.

Feature Engineering

The combination of 6 Pokémon each with their own stats and characteristics gave us many potential features to focus on. Such features included team-level stats known at the beginning of the game—such as speed, attack, defense, HP, and Pokémon types.

  • Type Diversity: The number of Pokémon with unique types on the team.
  • Advantage Statistic: Approximates the expected difference in total damage two opposing Pokémon would exchange in a one-on-one match.
  • Team Advantage: The cumulative sum of the Advantages for all 36 possible pairs of opposing Pokémon from each team.
  • Stat Boosters: The differential in the number of Pokémon on each team which know a stat-boosting move or have a stat-boosting ability.

Model Selection

We tested a number of different models on different subsets of features. For a baseline model, it was sensible to use a single-feature predictor based on the players' Elo rating differences. Beyond the baseline, we considered two types of logistic regressions (biased and unbiased), decision trees, random forests, HGBoost, and XGBoost models.

Each model was evaluated on its cross-validation accuracy and the symmetry of its confusion matrix. The baseline model was 52.1% accurate, and the unbiased logistic regression was 53.1% accurate; both were well-calibrated. The remaining models had varying levels of accuracy but were poorly calibrated, leading us to select the unbiased logistic regression as our final model.

Conclusion and Next Steps

Based on confusion matrices, accuracy, and cross-validation scores, the best model we found was the unbiased logistic regression with a final model test accuracy of 53.6%. This result is not significantly higher than a coin flip of 50%, leading us to conclude that the scaling performed by Pokémon Showdown for Generation 9 random battles is sufficiently balanced to ensure fair competition.

Next Steps
There remains potential to create a predictive model based on turn-by-turn analysis. Instead of relying on statistics determined pre-game, this future model would construct advantage stats for each turn and produce a win probability as a function of time.
